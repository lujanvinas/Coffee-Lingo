espresso

[eˈsprɛsso]

Straight and simple. A full-flavored, concentrated form of coffee that is served in single or double shots.

x

To make an espresso a small amount of nearly boiling water is forced under pressure (expressed) through finely-ground coffee beans. It is generally thick and rich, and has a foam with a creamy consistency called crema.

Did you know?

When you order "un caffé" in Italy, it is implied that it is an espresso.

x

americano

[ameriˈkaːno]

A watered-down single or double espresso, literally.

x

The drink consists of a single or double shot of espresso brewed with added water. Typically between 28 and 455 ml of hot water is added to the double espresso.

Did you know?

It is believed that the term "caffè Americano" has its origins in World War II when American soldiers in Italy would dilute espresso with hot water to approximate the coffee to which they were accustomed.

x

latte

[kaffeˈlatte]

Typically two shots of espresso served in a big mug, complete with steamed milk and a tiny bit of foam.

x

Caffè latte is typically prepared in a 240 mL cup with a single or double shot of espresso and filled with steamed milk.

Did you know?

In Italy, caffè latte is almost always prepared at home, for breakfast only.

x

cappuccino

[kapputˈtʃiːno]

Double shot of espresso, steamed milk and a lot of foam.

x

Cappuccino is typically composed of a double espresso shot and hot milk, with the surface topped with foamed milk.

Did you know?

In Italy it is very common to hear people refer to the cappuccino as "cappuccio".

x

macchiato

[kafˈfɛ mmakˈkjaːto]

"Stained" in Italian. Add a splash of steamed milk to a single or double espresso.

x

The espresso macchiato has the highest ratio of espresso to milk of any drink made with those ingredients. The intent is that the milk moderates, rather than overwhelms, the taste of the coffee while adding a touch of sweetness.

Did you know?

The equivalent of espresso macchiatto is "café cortado" in Spanish or "café pingado" in Portuguese.
